LLMs Can't Reason (And That's Okay)

February 3, 2026 · 12 min read · AI

The discourse cycle is familiar by now. Someone posts a benchmark showing that a frontier LLM fails at some seemingly simple logical reasoning task — the wolves and chickens river-crossing problem, a novel variant of the Wason selection task, grade-school arithmetic with unusual formatting. The replies split into two camps:

  • "See? LLMs just pattern-match. They fundamentally cannot reason."
  • "This benchmark is contrived. Real-world performance is fine."

Both camps are partly right. I want to do something more useful: steel-man each position, point out where they talk past each other, and then suggest what I think the productive research framing is.

The "LLMs Can't Reason" Camp Is Right About This

LLMs exhibit systematic failure modes that look nothing like how a reasoning agent would fail. A model that truly "understood" logic shouldn't fail on structurally identical problems just because the surface tokens changed. It shouldn't give confidently wrong answers on problems it got right one paragraph earlier. It shouldn't be so sensitive to whether you say "think step by step" before asking.

These aren't capability gaps you'd expect to close with scale alone. They suggest something architectural — a mismatch between how LLMs compute and what reasoning requires.

In my research, I've had to carefully distinguish between an LLM producing a correct-looking proof sketch and an LLM producing a correct proof sketch. The gap is not small.

The "Benchmarks Are Contrived" Camp Is Also Right

Most reasoning benchmarks are deeply weird from an ecological validity standpoint. Real programming tasks, real scientific analysis, real argument evaluation — these all involve reasoning under context, with relevant prior knowledge, in collaborative settings.

When I ask an LLM to help me think through a proof, it often does something genuinely useful: it reminds me of relevant lemmas I'd forgotten, suggests structurally analogous arguments, points out cases I'd missed. Is that "reasoning"? Maybe not in a formal sense, but it's useful cognitive scaffolding.

The question isn't whether LLMs can do textbook logic puzzles. It's whether they can be reliable thinking partners on the tasks that matter.

The Frame I Find Most Useful

Here's my preferred frame: LLMs are very good at approximate, context-sensitive pattern matching over vast knowledge. Reasoning (in the formal sense) requires precise, compositional rule application. These are different competencies.

The interesting question for my research area isn't "can LLMs reason" but rather: how can we compose LLMs with systems that can reason, to get the benefits of both?

This is the neurosymbolic hypothesis: use the LLM as a fast, heuristic search guide over a space of programs or proofs, and use a formal verifier to check correctness. The LLM's "approximate reasoning" narrows the search dramatically; the verifier ensures nothing incorrect slips through.

What This Means For My Work

My recent ICML paper on reflective prompting is one small piece of this puzzle: a structured prompting framework that leads LLMs to explicitly state intermediate conclusions and then verify them before proceeding. This reduces cascading errors in compositional tasks. But it's a band-aid, not a cure.

The bigger project — which I'm still figuring out how to frame as a PhD thesis — is about what architecture would actually support reliable reasoning in code-generation systems. My intuition is that the answer involves learned program search with symbolic guardrails, but I'll be honest: I don't have this figured out yet.
