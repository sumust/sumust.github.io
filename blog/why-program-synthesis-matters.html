Why Program Synthesis Still Matters (And Is Finally Taking Off)

March 12, 2026 · 8 min read · Research

For decades, the dream of program synthesis — telling a computer what you want and having it figure out how — has been one of the most ambitious goals in computer science. It has also been, for most of that time, mostly a dream.

I joined the program synthesis community as a PhD student three years ago, and even I sometimes wondered whether I'd picked a field perpetually "five years from maturity." But something has genuinely shifted. I want to try to articulate why — not just with AI hype, but with something more concrete.

What Synthesis Actually Is

First, a brief grounding. Program synthesis is the problem of automatically generating programs that satisfy a specification. That specification might be:

  • A set of input/output examples ("given [1,3,2], return [1,2,3]")
  • A logical formula (a formal spec in something like Coq or Dafny)
  • Natural language ("sort this list in ascending order")
  • A combination of all three

The classic view was that synthesis was a search problem: enumerate programs in some language until you find one that satisfies your spec. This worked for small, constrained domains — Excel formula synthesis (FlashFill), SQL query synthesis, small functional programs. For anything larger, the search space exploded exponentially and synthesis became intractable.

What Changed

Two things happened (roughly) simultaneously that changed the landscape:

1. LLMs became surprisingly good at code generation. Models like Codex, then GPT-4, then Gemini showed that statistical patterns in billions of lines of code could, shockingly, produce plausible implementations for complex specifications given in natural language. This wasn't synthesis in the traditional sense — there were no correctness guarantees — but it radically shifted what people thought was possible.

2. Neurosymbolic approaches started working. Researchers found ways to combine neural network "guessers" (which propose candidate programs quickly) with symbolic "verifiers" (which check correctness formally). The neural component shrinks the search space; the symbolic component ensures correctness. My own work on gradient-guided program repair is one example of this direction.

Why I'm Excited (And A Bit Anxious)

I'm excited because the hardest bottleneck in synthesis — the exponential search space — is finally cracking open. Neural guidance doesn't guarantee correctness, but it gets us to the right neighborhood dramatically faster.

The dream was always to have a collaborator that writes correct code with you. Not one that writes plausible-looking code at you.

I'm a bit anxious because there's a real risk the field bifurcates into "LLM code generation with vibes" and "symbolic synthesis for toy problems," and misses the productive middle ground. The interesting science is in making the two work together, with proper correctness properties, in interaction with a human who has partial, evolving intentions.

Where I Think This Goes

In five years, I expect synthesis tools to be a normal part of software development workflows. Not just autocomplete, but genuine interactive partners: you describe an API contract, the tool proposes an implementation, you critique it, it refines — with formal guarantees woven in throughout.

The key research challenges that remain, as I see them:

  • Specification acquisition: Most programs don't have formal specs. How do we elicit what a programmer actually wants, including their tacit knowledge and unstated invariants?
  • Interaction design: How do humans and synthesis systems collaborate efficiently? What feedback is cheap to give, what's expensive?
  • Scalability: Synthesis still struggles with large programs. Decomposition and compositionality are key.

I'll be writing more about each of these. For now: if you're a student wondering what PL research is exciting right now, synthesis is having a moment. Come join us.
