One Year In: Honest Reflections on a CS PhD

September 15, 2024 · 6 min read · PhD Life

A year ago, I moved to Pittsburgh with two suitcases, a brand-new CMU student ID, and a head full of grand research visions. I was going to solve program synthesis. I had a five-year plan.

Here is my current plan: "figure it out."

That's not resignation — it's actually progress. Here's what I learned.

What Surprised Me (Good)

The community is warm. I expected academia to be competitive and cold — everyone guarding their ideas, racing to scoop. The actual experience has been mostly the opposite. People share pre-prints generously, read your papers and give you feedback, and genuinely want to see good work happen regardless of who does it.

The work is actually fun. I knew I liked research, but I didn't expect to feel this consistently engaged. Even the slog of debugging an experiment at 1am has a quality of aliveness that a lot of engineering work doesn't, for me. You're genuinely not sure what you'll find.

My advisor is a real person. I had anxiety about the advisor-student relationship — I'd heard horror stories. Jordan has been patient, honest, and hasn't once made me feel stupid for not knowing something. This is not guaranteed; I got lucky and I know it.

What Surprised Me (Less Good)

How much of research is just... waiting. Waiting for experiments to run. Waiting for reviewer scores. Waiting for your brain to have the idea you need. I am not naturally patient. This has been a growth area.

The first-year coursework is not the bottleneck you think it is. I spent the first semester worrying about grades in courses. Your research output is what matters. The courses matter instrumentally — you need the foundations — but optimizing for grades is a trap.

Imposter syndrome is real and persistent. Even after a paper acceptance, the voice saying "you just got lucky, they'll figure you out" doesn't go away. I've started treating it like weather: acknowledge it, and go do the work anyway.

What I Got Wrong

My biggest mistake was trying to do too many things at once. I had three active research threads going in year one. One turned into a paper. The other two were basically wasted effort — not because the ideas were bad, but because I spread myself too thin to go deep on any of them. If I could do it again: pick one thing, go deep, and let the depth generate breadth naturally.

What I'd Tell My Past Self

Take the reading group seriously. The weekly discussion of other people's papers is where you develop taste — the ability to know which problems are important and which results are significant. This is the most important skill in research and it's developed slowly over many conversations.

Talk to more senior students. They know which courses are actually useful, which tools are standard, how the advisor works, what the department culture is like. Don't figure it out alone when you don't have to.

Protect your personal life. Research will expand to fill all available time if you let it. It will also contract fine if you set boundaries. Go climbing. Call your family. Sleep eight hours. You will not be less productive; you will be more sustainable.

Year two started last week. I have one clear research direction, a collaborator I trust, and a much better espresso setup than I had a year ago. Not bad.
